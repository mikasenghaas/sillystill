Film photography is a popular medium for capturing images due to its unique visual characteristics which are difficult to replicate with digital cameras. Film images exhibit unique grain patterns, color palettes, and halation effects around light sources. These visual effects are the result of complex physical properties of film, such as its chemical properties and the way that lightwaves interact with the light-sensitive particles in the film strip. Aestetically and artistically people enjoy the look of film photography, but the process of acquiring film and developing it is time consuming and expensive. Additionally, the development of a given film exposure is not guaranteed to be successful and there is a time delay between taking the photo, bringing the film to a darkroom for development, and finally scanning the negatives. 

Therefore, there is a need for a digital solution that can recreate the visual effects of film photography in real-time. This would allow for the rapid and easy creation of film-like visuals without the need for expensive film development. Such a solution would be useful for photographers who want to experiment with film photography without the cost and time commitment of developing film. Our approach works specifically with Cinestill 800T and its unique appearance.

Available solutions for recreating film affect involve directly expressing the physical properties of film using statistical models. By modelling pixel patterns of noise, halation, and color distortions, one can directly attempt to recreate the visual effects of film. Such approaches like \cite{film-grain-rendering} have been proposed to model the grain and noise patterns of film using physically realistic models. Grain is then rendered using a Monte Carlo simulation to determine the output of each pixel.

However, analytically modeling film is difficult. Cinestill 800T film, like most film formats, is a sheet of plastic coated with a layer of light-sensitive silver hallide crystals. When light enters the camera and hits the film, the particles react to the light and create a latent image. The film is then developed in a darkroom using a series of chemical baths which react with the particles in the film to create a visible image. The visual effects of film are therefore the result of complex interplay of physical processes involving that are difficult to capture with simple mathematical models.

Even if accurate mathematical models of film grain, halation, and color hue are defined, using such models to directly process each pixel in an image is computationally expensive and slow. The results of mathematical models of film grain are not always visually pleasing as they fail to capture the complexity of the film affect. A high quality digital image with height and width dimensions on the order of thousands would require several minutes to simulate with direct pixel manipulation. \textcolor{red}{Cite something for this claim!! }

Ideally, a digital image could be processed into its film equivalent on the order of seconds to acheive results that are visually pleasing and indistinguishable from a true film image. 

Only visual film affects which are understood from first principles and can be modeled with closed-form statistical models can be encoded directly. Deep learning models have the ability to learn complex mappings and patterns not necessarily perceivable by the human visual system. Thus, one can hope that a deep learning model could achieve results which appear more authentic to the human eye. However, the problem of recreating film affect has not been directly addressed by deep learning literature. Related computational photography problems include camera raw-to-raw mappings, quality enhancement, greyscale colorisation, and style transfer. As none have been directly applied to the problem of film affect, we examined the techniques employed and whether they could be efficiently adapted to our task and to inform our final approach.

We propose a Unet-like architecture trained on a paired dataset of digital and Cinestill 800T photographs of the same scene, captured at the same angle with a fixed tripod. Digital images are captured on G Sony camera with a 40mm lens with mostly F8 aperture and automatic shutter speed. Cinestill 800T film images were captures on a Nikon F3 camera with a 55mm lens with mostly F8 aperture and automatic shutter speed as well. For very bright scenes, aperture was adjusted up to F32. The dataset was captured in a variety of lighting conditions, including indoor and outdoor scenes around Lausanne, Switzerland. Due to imperfectly aligned digital and film image pairs and the difference in lens widths, we performed image alignment and luminance histogram matching of pairs to preprocess the dataset.

We restrain this work to only solutions involving deep learning. Assuming that the task of recreating film visual affects can be accomplished using purely learning methods, we set out to explore the feasibility of this approach. We acknowledge that the combination of deep learning approaches with direct statistical models of film grain, halation, and color hue may be an overall more effective approach, but we do not explore this in this work. We present our dataset, model architecture, training process, and results in the following sections.
